Abstract

Severe air pollution has become a global concern, and there is a pressing need to develop effective and efficient air filters for removing airborne particulate matters (PMs). In this work, a highly permeable poly(ether sulfone) (PES) based hollow fiber membrane was developed via a one-step dry-jet wet spinning. For the first time, a hollow fiber membrane was used in removing the ultrafine particles (PMs with aerodynamic equivalent diameters of less than 100 nm) in PM2.5. The novel air filter was designed to possess the synergistic advantages of porous filters and fibrous filters with a sievelike outer surface and a fibrouslike porous substrate. A filtration efficiency of higher than 99.995% could be easily achieved when the self-support hollow fiber was challenged with less than 300 nm particulates. Without losses of the structural advantages, we have demonstrated that the permeation properties of the hollow fiber membrane can be facilely tailored via manipulation of the dope and bore fluid formulations. Various cleaning strategies were explored to regenerate the membrane performance after fouling. Both water rinse and backwash showed effectiveness to restore the membrane permeance for repetitive usage.

Introduction

As the most severe urban pollutants in air, suspended particulate matters (PMs) have been of particular concern in the recent decades due to their influence on climates and human health.(1) PMs may be classified into many size categories as illustrated in Figure 1(a), and numerous epidemiological studies have revealed that particle sizes are directly associated with their potentials for causing health problems.(2, 3) The finer PMs exhibit longer residence time in air and larger specific surfaces, making them viable carriers for other pollutants, thus exaggerating their threat to human health.(4-7) They can lodge deeply into human respiratory systems upon inhalation, and even a short-term exposure could lead to risks of cardiopulmonary morbidity and mortality as well as reduced life expectancies.(5, 8-12) The growing awareness of health threat from the finer PMs and the adverse impact of air pollution on people’s lives have led to progressively stringent regulations of PMs, which exerts enormous pressure on the need for ultrafine PMs mitigations.(13)

Figure 1. (a) Size comparison of PMs against fine beach sand and human hair and illustrations of (b) porous air filter and (c) fibrous air filter.

At the moment, there are two types of commercially available air filters; namely, porous membrane filters and fibrous filters.(14-16) The porous type of filter acts like a sieve, which excludes the particles based on the size-exclusion mechanism (Figure 1(b)). To achieve a high filtration efficiency against particles covering a wide size range, the surface pores need to be sufficiently small to prevent the finest PMs from penetrating through. This not only limits the surface porosity but also creates excessive pressure drop and leads to high clogging rates.(17) In contrast, a fibrous filter is constructed from fibers of controllable diameters, arranged into random layers and stacked onto one another (Figure 1(c)). After the particle-laden air flows through the filter, various sizes of PMs are captured due to impaction, interception, and diffusion onto the fibers.(18) However, the characteristics of symmetric structure and deep bed filtrations make the penetration of PM possible, causing the cleaning and reuse of this type of filters more challenging.(14, 19-21) Notable efforts have been devoted to develop electrospun nanofibers with extremely fine diameters as fibrous filters for air filtration in the past decade and significant progress can be evidenced from their works.(22-30) However, the attempts to make nanofibers with extremely fine diameters could jeopardize the filters’ robustness; a substrate is often required to provide the mechanical support.(31, 32) This creates extra difficulties in filter productions and makes the products susceptible to detachment.(20, 21, 24, 25) Besides, developing fibrous filters with a uniform structure and stable cavity is still a formidable challenge.(14, 22, 33)
Comparing with the flat-sheet nature of the conventional air filters, hollow fiber membranes have the advantages of self-support structure and high packing density for module fabrication.(34) Without the need of additional separators and being tactfully pleated, a large filtration area could be easily achieved by simply assembling the strawlike hollow fibers in a module. Moreover, by controlling the spinning parameters during fabrication, flexible designs of hollow fiber membranes with various geometries and morphologies could be achieved.(35-37) As such, hollow fiber membranes have been widely explored and adopted for versatile applications, including water treatment, osmotic power generation, and gas separation.(38-44) However, despite the numerous advantages, there was rarely any work focusing on the development of hollow fiber membranes for air filtration. In this work, a novel hollow fiber membrane comprising the structural advantages of both porous filters and fibrous filters is designed to remove the submicron particles in PM2.5. The structure of the developed hollow fibers may confer the filters with additional benefits such as reduced particle penetration, ease of cleaning, and ultimately extended service life. Therefore, the aims of this study are to 1) design a flexible hollow fiber membrane for ultrafine particles removal through controlling various spinning parameters; 2) evaluate air filtration performance of the developed hollow fiber membrane and particle penetration behaviors across the membrane; and 3) develop cleaning strategies to extend its service life for repetitive usage. This study may serve as a pioneer work to demonstrate the feasibility of using the developed hollow fibers for air filtration and provide tangible guidance on the cleaning strategies for long-term usage.

Materials and Methods

Fabrication of the Hollow Fiber Membranes
The hollow fiber membranes were fabricated by the dry-jet wet spinning method using a dual-channel spinneret. Table S1 lists the spinning conditions of the PES hollow fiber membranes. The detailed procedures for the dope preparation and the spinning process are described in the Supporting Information. The spinning setup and the spinneret are illustrated in Figure S1.
Characterizations
The morphology of the hollow fiber membranes was observed by a field emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM JEOL, JSM-6700LV). Prior to observation, the fiber samples were fractured in liquid nitrogen and sputtered with platinum using a JEOL JFC-1200 ion sputtering device.
Permeation Properties of the Hollow Fiber Membranes
The nitrogen permeance was measured as a primary indication of air transport resistance. Nitrogen gas was fed into the permeation tube, and in contact with the fiber outer surface, the flow rate permeated out from the lumen side was measured with a universal gas flow meter (ADM1000, Agilent Technologies Inc.) under a transmembrane pressure of 2 psi. The average of 5 readings was recorded.
Pure water permeability (PWP) (Lm–2 h–1 bar–1 or LMH bar–1) of hollow fibers provides an indirect measurement of the overall fiber porosity and the feasibility of backwash. PWP of the membranes was determined by flowing deionized water from the shell side of the module under a transmembrane pressure of 0.25 bar. The average of 4 measurements was reported for each sample.
Aerosol Filtration Experiment
Figure 2 shows the experimental setup for evaluating the filtration efficiency of the membrane filter, including an aerosol generation system, a hollow fiber module, and a particle detection system. Ammonium sulfate aerosols, one of the major constituents of PMs, were generated by an Atomizer (Model 3076, TSI) to simulate the ambient submicron PMs.(4, 45) Detailed descriptions of the aerosol generation and operating conditions can be found in the Supporting Information.

Figure 2. Schematic diagram of the experimental setup for particulate generation, filtration, and measurement system.

The size distributions of PMs in the air were measured using a Scanning Mobility Particle Sizer (SMPS 3936, TSI). Those particles with sizes in between 10 and 334 nm were classified by a Differential Mobility Analyzer (DMA 3081, TSI) and counted by a Water-Condensational Particle Counter (WCPC 3782, TSI).(46) The air before and after the membrane filters was sampled, and the filtration efficiency of the membrane filter was determined usingwhere C0 and C correspond to the particle number concentrations before and after the membrane filters.(47) The face velocity was kept at 2.6 cm/s during the outside-in filtration configuration as the filtration efficiency for particles smaller than 100 nm was shown to be independent of humidity under this operating condition.(48)
Elemental Analysis of Membrane Surface and Cross Section after Filtration
To distinguish the aerosol sources from the membrane materials, aerosol generated from sodium chloride solution was used to investigate the penetration behavior of PMs across the hollow fiber membranes.(14, 23) The size distribution of the aerosols generated using sodium chloride was provided in Figure S2. The elemental analyses on inner and outer surfaces of the membranes before and after filtration were performed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Kratos Analytical). Elemental analyses on the fiber cross section after filtration were conducted by a scanning electron microscopy (JSM-5600LV) equipped with an Oxford INCA energy dispersion X-ray system (SEM-EDX). The acquisition time for EDX line-scan was 180 s.
Regeneration Strategies for Fouled Membranes
After the hollow fiber membranes were applied for air filtration, the fouled membranes were cleaned by several physical methods to regenerate their performances. Detailed procedures for air back purge, water backwash, and water rinsing can be found in the Supporting Information. To examine the efficacy of various regeneration methods, the pressure drop (ΔP) across the modules was continuously monitored using a differential pressure transmitter (EJA110E, Yokogawa), and the flow rate of the permeated air (F) was recorded with a high accuracy whisper mass flow meter (MW-5 SLPM, Alicat). Both sets of data were recorded by an online recorder (FX1006, Yokogawa), and then the normalized pressure drop (ΔP/F) and the permeance were calculated.

Results and Discussion

Tuning the Permeation Properties of Hollow Fiber Membranes
One advantage of hollow fiber membranes is that their inner and outer layer structures as well as their permeation properties can be facilely tuned via manipulation of multiple spinning parameters in a one-step spinning. We have varied (1) the polymer concentration, (2) PVP amount, and (3) water amount in the bore fluid, and Table S2 tabulates the geometrical properties and overall porosities of the resultant fibers spun under different conditions. All membranes possess similar dimensions with the inner and outer diameters around 900 and 1200 μm, respectively, and thicknesses around 140 μm. They exhibit similar morphologies (not shown here) and have relatively high porosities of larger than 75%.
Figure 3 shows the PWP values and gas permeance of the membranes as a function of polymer and PVP concentrations in the spinning dopes and water content in the bore fluid. A highly porous and permeable membrane is always preferred to reduce the filter resistance to air flow. Both PWP and gas permeance increase when the polymer concentration decreases from 19% to 16% as the membranes become more porous. A low polymer concentration in spinning solutions would lead to a low degree of polymer chain entanglement during phase inversion, thus broaden the flow channels for water and air transport. In addition, manipulation of the PVP amount in dopes could further alter the permeation properties due to the pore former nature of PVP. Since PVP would eventually be washed out during the post-treatment process, adding a large amount of PVP in dopes would result in membranes with a high porosity and therefore enhance the permeability. Besides, bore fluid composition also affects the membrane formation. Water is a strong nonsolvent to PES as compared to the mixture of TEG and NMP. Thus, a decrease in water content in bore fluid would induce a more porous inner surface.(49) As a result, the PWP and gas permeance of the membranes increase. In view of the low operation pressure of airflow in actual applications, the membrane with the highest PWP and gas permeability (condition G) is chosen for further investigations in the air filtration experiments despite the slight decreases in mechanical properties (Table S3(a)).

Figure 3. Nitrogen permeance and PWP of hollow fibers spun from dopes with (a) different polymer concentrations, (b) different PVP amounts, and (c) different water amounts in bore fluid.

Figure 4 displays the membrane morphology spun from condition G. It has an open-cell interconnected macrovoid-free structure that would ensure the filter not only with good mechanical integrity but also minimal transport resistance.(34) The membrane possesses a relatively asymmetric cross-section morphology consisting of a fully porous substrate and an ultrathin selective outer layer with submicron pores on the surface. This structure is desirable for air filtration as it has the following benefits during outside-in filtration (i.e., the particle-laden air flows in from the shell side, and the purified air permeates out from the inner lumen side, as illustrated in Figure 5(a)): (1) The sievelike outer surface acts like the porous filter (Figure 1(b)) to physically sieve out those relatively larger particles via size exclusion. The average pore size on the membrane surface is approximately 95.7 ± 32.2 nm as analyzed by Image-Pro Plus. Figure 5(c) shows the deposition state of particles on the membrane outer surface after outside-in filtration. The inner surface looks the same as the pristine membrane as shown in Figure 4(d) without noticeable changes. Compared to other filtration mechanisms where the filtration efficiencies are highly dependent on external factors such as air velocity and relative humidity, the use of physical sieving is the safest strategy in air filter designs as it assures complete capture of particles larger than the pore openings regardless of the operating conditions.(14, 19, 22, 50) (2) The interconnected network underlying the outer surface provides a second layer of fibrous filter (Figure 1(c)) to provide protection against those penetrated ultrafine particles. The spongelike cross section provides tortuous pathways which could trap those ultrafine PMs via impaction, interception, and diffusion, leading to a superior removal efficiency.(26, 27) A schematic illustration of the filtration process with the developed membrane is shown in Figure 5(b).

Figure 4. FESEM images of the hollow fiber membrane spun from condition G which has the highest permeability.

Figure 5. (a) Illustration of the hollow fiber module and filtration configuration, (b) side view of the membrane filter and schematic illustration of the filtration process, and (c) the FESEM image of the membrane outer surface after the ammonium sulfate filtration.

Additionally, the small pores on the outer layer can also help to extend the filter life because the particle loading on the underlying network can be reduced. As a result, the hollow fibers can be cleaned up easier compared to those fibrous filters in which PMs are trapped inside the fibrous network via deep bed filtration.(19, 51) Overall, the synergistic contributions of multiple filtration mechanisms across the whole cross section have conferred the newly developed hollow fiber membrane with great potentials for removing the submicron PMs, which will be further explored in the next section.
Filtration Performance of the Hollow Fiber Membranes
The fabricated hollow fiber membrane with the highest permeance is evaluated for air filtration performance. XPS spectra of the inner and outer surfaces of the pristine membrane and the fouled membranes after 24-h outside-in and inside-out filtration tests are shown in Figure 6, and quantitative analyses of the elements on the skin layers after different filtration durations can be found in Table S4. Though the absolute amounts of Na and Cl on the surfaces facing the feed air vary with the filtration time, the absence of Na and Cl peaks on the other side of the membrane indicates that there is no penetration of PMs across the membrane to the other side for both cases. Compared to the pristine membrane, the appearance of Na and Cl signals on the surfaces facing the feed stream (i.e., the outer surface under outside-in tests or the inner surface under inside-out tests) proves that a significant amount of particles is captured on the surfaces. The results show that the newly developed membrane can be used as a perfect barrier to the ultrafine particles in PM2.5.

Figure 6. XPS spectra of the outer and inner surfaces of (a) the pristine membranes, (b) fouled membranes after outside-in filtration, and (c) fouled membranes after inside-out filtration.

To verify that the outer surface has effectively helped to reduce the particle penetration, the results of EDX line scans across the membrane cross sections after the outside-in and inside-out filtration are compared in Figure 7. Interestingly, distinct Na and Cl peaks on the outer edge can be observed after the outside-in filtration, suggesting the accumulation of particles near the outer surface. In contrast, the Na and Cl signals show gradual decreasing intensity from the inner edge toward the outer edge after the inside-out filtration because the inner surface has larger pores for the penetration and accumulation of finer sodium chloride particles. The comparison elucidates the structural advantages of the hollow fiber which make it an attractive candidate for air filtration.

Figure 7. SEM-EDX line scans of the cross sections of hollow fiber membranes after (a) outside-in filtration and (b) inside-out filtration.

To quantitatively determine the filtration efficiency of the newly developed membrane filter, ammonium sulfate particles are generated from a 0.01 g/L solution. The size distributions of the aerosols before and after filtration are shown in Figure S3. A majority of the feed particles (>95% in number) are ultrafine PMs with a mean diameter of 53 nm (Table 1). After outside-in filtrations, there are virtually no PMs smaller than 50 nm in the filtrated air, whereas only a negligible amount of relatively larger particles is present in the permeate (as evidenced from Figure S3 and comparison in Table 1). The former may be attributed to the fact that (1) small particles are more likely to be trapped via the tortuous paths across the membrane due to Brownian diffusion,(20, 47) and (2) fine particulates would experience a strong van der Waals force when their sizes are comparable to the diameter of the interconnected fibrous network.(28, 52) Since the particulates with a size larger than the surface openings would be blocked by the outer surface, the relatively larger particles in the permeate may originate from the agglomeration and coalescence of the finer particulates when they travel through the membrane cross section.(15) Table 1 summarizes the aerosol statistics of the feed and permeate after the outside-in filtration. Clearly, the newly developed hollow fiber has achieved an ultrahigh particle removal efficiency of over 99.995% for particles smaller than 0.3 μm which has exceeded the current standard for the commercially available high-efficiency particulate arrestance (HEPA) filters (99.97% for particle >0.3 μm).(53) A comparison table can be found in Table S5 which compares the hollow fiber performance for PM2.5 removal with the nanofibers in the recent literature.(15, 16, 22, 26, 28, 29)

Regeneration of the Membrane Performance
Another challenge to overcome is the need of replacing air filters after long time usage, which would in turn escalate the costs. Therefore, simple and effective cleaning strategies are essential to regenerate the gas permeance of the fouled membranes. To examine the potential of the newly developed hollow fiber for repetitive usage, the effectiveness of various cleaning methods is studied, including air back purge, water rinse, and water backwash.
Figure 8(a) shows the normalized pressure drop in terms of millibar/standard liter per minute (mbar/slpm) across the hollow fiber membranes and the changes in membrane permeance before filtrations and after filtrations as well as after cleaning. Compared with the pristine membrane modules, the normalized pressure drop increases significantly, and the fiber experiences noticeable decreases in the permeance after filtration. This implies a higher airflow resistance in the membrane after filtration which is likely caused by fouling and clogging. Similar phenomena have been reported for other air filters.(28, 54, 55) Air back purge across the membrane shows little impact on recovering the membrane permeance, and the outer surface is still contaminated with particles after air back purge (Figure 8(b)). The high affinity between the hygroscopic ammonium sulfate particles and the membranes could have probably led to the difficulties in regenerating membrane permeance by air purge.(24, 56) In contrast, both water rinse on the membrane and backwash across the membrane show effectiveness, as confirmed by the FESEM images of membrane outer surfaces after cleaning in Figure 8(c),(d). However, the permeance after water rinse is slightly lower than that of the pristine membrane module. This could be attributed to the gradual deposition of the penetrated particulates on the membrane interstices which results in a certain degree of irreversible fouling by simple surface rinse. In comparison, water backwash can remarkably recover the membrane permeance to the initial value. One plausible explanation is that the hydraulic pressure during the backwash induces a water flow that could carry away the accumulated foulants trapped inside the membranes.(57)

Figure 8. (a) Normalized pressure drop and permeance changes across the pristine, fouled, and cleaned membranes. FESEM images of outer surfaces after (b) air back purge, (c) water rinse, and (d) water backwash.

Multicycle air filtration tests were conducted to investigate the long-term performance of the membrane filter. To reduce water penetration and shorten the downtime, water rinse was adopted in the multiple filtration-clean cycles, after which the fiber was dried thoroughly by air blowing. The normalized pressure drop as a function of time during the filtration-rinse-dry cycle is plotted in Figure 9. Three clogging stages can be observed in each cycle with characteristics similar to Nuclepore filters which have circular pores normal to the surfaces.(54, 58) First, pressure increases gradually with time due to the decrease of the effective pore size caused by particle deposition on the surface. After that, a sharp increase in pressure drop is observed resulting from the rapid pore filling. Lastly, a sluggish growth of the pressure drop is noticed because of the slowly growing particulate layer. The trend corroborates the resemblance of the membrane outer surface to a porous type filter. The significant dive in pressure drop after each rinsing-drying process clearly reflects the efficacy of water rinse; however, the progressively increased baseline for pressure drop (indicated by the dotted curve) implies that the irreversible part of clogging that could not be mitigated by water rinse.

Figure 9. Normalized pressure drop (●) and filtration efficiency (▲) during filtration-rinse-dry cycles.

It is worth mentioning that the filtration efficiency of the membrane remains well above 99.995% during the filtration cycles without obvious deterioration in the PMs removal performance (Figure 9). Comparing with HEPA, where the trapped moisture might interfere with the filter structure and three wetting and drying cycles were sufficient to cause deterioration in its filtration efficiency,(59) the structures of the hollow fiber membranes remain intact after water rinse as compared with the pristine membrane, and their mechanical properties are not affected as shown in Table S3(b). This indicates that the stability of the membrane during PMs filtration is not jeopardized by the long-term operation and cleaning.
